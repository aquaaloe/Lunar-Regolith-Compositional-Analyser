Apollo / Luna Sample Suite

This instrument permits the examination of estimated lunar regolith composition, maturity, and grain-size distribution for arbitrary near-side localities, integrated over a user-specified depth column. Compositional estimates are derived by inverse-distance-weighted interpolation from Apollo 11, 12, 14, 15, 16, 17 and Luna 16, 20, 24 returned-sample analyses. Depth-dependent quantities (agglutinate fraction, Is/FeO maturity index) follow empirical decay models calibrated to Apollo core-tube data. Major-oxide composition is treated as depth-invariant within the gardened zone (0–30 cm). Select a locality upon the hemispheric map; adjust the depth of analysis to obtain column-averaged maturity values.

Spatial Interpolationv.b

For any clicked selenographic coordinate, properties are estimated by inverse-distance-weighted (IDW) interpolation from the N = 5 nearest sample-return sites.

Distance metric. Great-circle (angular) distance on a spherical Moon (R = 1737.4 km). Computed via the Haversine formula.

Weighting. Each site receives weight wi = 1/di², where di is the angular distance in degrees (floored at 0.1° to prevent singularity). The interpolated value of any property P is:

P̂ = Σ wi Pi / Σ wi

Cross-type penalty. When a sample site’s terrain type (mare or highland) differs from the clicked locality’s classification, its effective distance is inflated 3×. This prevents unrealistic blending across sharp mare–highland contacts (e.g., an Apollo 16 highland value contaminating a nearby mare estimate). The “margin” locality type blends freely with both.

All interpolated distributions (grain size, modal abundances) are renormalized to sum to 100% after weighting. This is a conservative spatial model — values degrade significantly beyond ~30° from any sample site. With only 9 anchor points, this is an interpolation, not a map.

Uncertainty estimation. For each interpolated quantity, a weighted standard deviation is reported as a ± range, measuring inter-site dispersion among contributing samples:

σ = √( Σ wi (Pi − P̂)² / Σ wi )

This quantifies how much the nearest sample sites disagree at a given location. Near a sample-return site, σ approaches zero (one site dominates). At distant or boundary locations, σ grows as dissimilar sites contribute more equally. For depth-dependent quantities (Is/FeO, agglutinate fraction), σ is propagated through the same exponential decay integration used for the mean, which is mathematically valid because the decay is a multiplicative scaling that factors out of the variance.

These ± values reflect spatial dispersion among a small number of anchor sites (N ≤ 5), not formal analytical uncertainties or prediction intervals. With only 9 total sites, they should be interpreted as indicative of interpolation confidence rather than rigorous error bounds.

Terrane Classificationv.c

The instrument classifies each clicked point into one of three major geochemical terranes defined by Jolliff et al. (2000):

Procellarum KREEP Terrane (PKT) — elevated Th (>3.5 ppm), K, and rare-earth elements, centered on Oceanus Procellarum and Mare Imbrium. Defined by a rough polygon boundary approximating Jolliff et al. (2000) Fig. 1.

South Pole–Aitken Terrane (SPA) — lower Al2O3 and elevated FeO relative to typical highlands, possibly exposing mantle material. Only the near-limb edge is visible on the nearside map.

Feldspathic Highlands Terrane (FHT) — the default classification for points outside PKT and SPA boundaries. Low FeO, high Al2O3.

Terrane assignment uses point-in-polygon testing against approximate boundary coordinates. For mare localities, the TiO2 content is further classified using Neal & Taylor (1992) thresholds: High-Ti (>6 wt%), Low-Ti (1–6 wt%), Very-Low-Ti (<1 wt%).

Depth-Dependent Maturity Modelv.d

Space-weathering effects (maturity) are strongest at the surface and decay with depth. The instrument models this as an exponential decay:

Is/FeO(d) = Is/FeOsurface · e−kd

where k = 0.04 cm−1 and d is depth in centimetres. The same decay applies to agglutinate fraction. This yields:

d = 0 cm: 100% of surface value
d = 10 cm: ~67% of surface value
d = 25 cm: ~37% of surface value
d = 50 cm: ~14% of surface value

Calibration. The decay constant k was fitted to the Apollo 15 deep drill core (samples 15001–15006, 240 cm total depth), which provides the longest continuous regolith profile. Is/FeO drops from ~60 at the surface to ~15 at depth (Morris 1978, Fig. 5). Apollo 16 cores (60001–60004) show a consistent profile.

Column averaging. For any user-selected integration depth D, the instrument reports the column-averaged value computed by trapezoidal numerical integration over [0, D] with 24 equal steps.

Major-oxide composition is treated as depth-invariant in the gardened zone (0–~50 cm). The regolith is vertically well-mixed on decimetre scales for major elements. Source: McKay et al. (1991).

Maturity classification follows Morris (1978): Mature (Is/FeO ≥ 60), Submature (30–60), Immature (<30).

Grain-Size Distribution Modelv.e

Surface distribution. Grain-size data for each site are stored as volume-percent in six Wentworth-scale bins: <20, 20–50, 50–100, 100–250, 250–500, and >500 μm. Source: Graf (1993) NASA RP-1265, which compiled sieve analyses for all Apollo and Luna soil samples. Spatial interpolation follows the same IDW scheme as composition (§ V.b).

Depth adjustment. While McKay et al. (1991) found no systematic grain-size trend with depth in the upper regolith, there is an indirect effect: agglutinates are preferentially concentrated in the finer size fractions (<90 μm). As agglutinate content decreases with depth (§ V.d), the fine fraction subtly shrinks and coarser fractions grow.

The model removes material from the three finest bins proportional to agglutinate loss, using empirically-derived bin weights (McKay et al. 1974, Fig. 3):

<20 μm: 40% of agglutinate mass
20–50 μm: 40% of agglutinate mass
50–100 μm: 20% of agglutinate mass
>100 μm: 0% (agglutinates rarely exceed ~100 μm)

Removed material is redistributed proportionally into the three coarsest bins, and the distribution is renormalized. This produces a subtle shift of a few vol% at 30 cm depth — consistent with the secondary nature of this effect.

Particle Type Modal Abundancesv.f

The instrument reports modal abundances in four particle-type categories (vol%, 90–150 μm sieve fraction):

Agglutinates — glass-bonded aggregates of smaller grains formed by micrometeorite impacts. Irregular, vesicular morphology. Dominant maturity indicator.

Lithic fragments — rock fragments (basalt, breccia, anorthosite). Angular to subangular.

Mineral fragments — free mineral grains (plagioclase, pyroxene, olivine, ilmenite). Angular, reflecting crystal habit.

Glass — impact glass (irregular, splashy) and volcanic glass beads (spherical). Reported combined as most published modal data do not separate them.

Source: Heiken et al. (1991) Table 7.9; Papike, Simon & Laul (1982) Table 4. Values are IDW-interpolated (§ V.b) and renormalized.

Depth model. Agglutinate fraction decays with depth using the same exponential as Is/FeO (§ V.d). Glass is held constant (impact and volcanic origins are not maturity-dependent). Lost agglutinate material is redistributed proportionally into lithic and mineral fragments. Column averaging uses trapezoidal integration.

Known Limitationsv.g

This instrument represents the most comprehensive aggregation of returned lunar soil data available. The Apollo (11, 12, 14, 15, 16, 17) and Luna (16, 20, 24) missions remain the sole source of ground-truth regolith samples from the lunar surface. The limitations enumerated below are therefore fundamental to the present state of lunar science—most can only be resolved through future landed missions and sample-return campaigns.

No pyroclastic glass deposits. Dark mantle deposits (e.g., Aristarchus Plateau, Rima Bode) and localized pyroclastic glasses (Apollo 17 orange soil, Apollo 15 green glass) have distinctive Fe/Ti-rich compositions not captured by the bulk-soil database. Characterising these deposits requires in-situ sampling at pyroclastic source vents.

No permanently shadowed region (PSR) volatiles. Cold-trapped H2O, OH, and other volatiles in polar PSRs are not modelled. No sample has ever been returned from a PSR; resolving this awaits missions such as VIPER or Lunar Polar Exploration.

No magnetic anomaly swirls. Anomalous maturity patterns at locations such as Reiner Gamma are not represented. These features have never been sampled directly; their effect on regolith properties remains constrained only by remote sensing.

No farside sample coverage. All nine sample-return sites are on the nearside. The farside—including the South Pole–Aitken basin, the largest recognised impact structure on the Moon—has no returned-sample ground truth. China’s Chang’e 5 (2020) returned nearside mare material; Chang’e 6 (2024) delivered the first farside samples, but published bulk-soil analyses are not yet available for integration.

Spatial coverage is sparse. Nine sites across a body 3,474 km in diameter yield an average spacing of ~15° between anchor points. Points more than ~30° from any sample site are poorly constrained extrapolations. Improving spatial resolution requires additional landed missions at geologically diverse localities.

No embedded remote-sensing data. Orbital datasets (Lunar Prospector GRS, Clementine spectral reflectance) could supplement the sample-derived model with continuous spatial coverage, but have not yet been integrated. All values are interpolations from the returned-sample database alone.

Uncertainty ranges are indicative. The reported ± values are weighted standard deviations reflecting inter-site dispersion among contributing samples (see § V.b). With only 9 anchor sites and N ≤ 5 contributors per query, these are not formal prediction intervals—they are the best estimate achievable from the existing sample suite.

Depth model is simplified. Real regolith cores show discrete strata, buried palaeosurfaces, and non-monotonic property variations. The smooth exponential decay used here is calibrated to the few deep drill cores returned (Apollo 15, 16, 17). Resolving true stratigraphic complexity requires in-situ drilling and core logging at new sites.

Single representative soil per site. Each mission is represented by one bulk-soil analysis. In reality, soil composition varies at metre to kilometre scales within each landing ellipse. Multiple samples per site exist in the literature, but a single best-characterised reference soil was selected for each to maintain consistency. Capturing intra-site variability would require a substantially expanded database drawing on all available sample splits.
